What your child will learn

How to make things that actually work.

Every project is real — something to build, test, break, and fix. The skills that emerge are computational, creative, and deeply practical.

🧠

Computational thinking

Breaking a problem into steps, spotting patterns, and designing a repeatable solution — the core skill underneath every programming language and every engineering discipline.

⚡

Electronics & circuits

Real sensors, motors, LEDs, and wiring. Children understand electricity before they automate it, so the code they write makes physical sense.

💻

Coding — Scratch, Arduino & Python

Each level progresses from visual block coding to written code. By the Maker track, children write functional Python and can explain what every line does.

🔧

Debugging & problem-solving

When the robot doesn't do what the code says, children learn to investigate rather than panic. Debugging is a skill — and one of the most transferable ones we teach.

FAQ

Common questions

Does my child need any coding experience?

No. The Junior track starts from zero — no assumed knowledge of code, electronics, or even computers. Children who already have some coding experience are placed in Builder or Maker based on a short assessment.

What equipment is provided?

All equipment is provided by Polaris — LEGO Mindstorms sets, Arduino kits, components, laptops, and everything else. Children bring nothing except curiosity. Equipment stays at the studio.

How do children move between tracks?

When they are genuinely ready, not when the year ends. David tracks each child's progress session by session. When a child has mastered the current level, we talk to parents and move them up — sometimes mid-term.

Is there a showcase at the end of the year?

Yes — an annual public showcase in spring where children present working projects to an audience of parents and guests. Maker track students also write a short technical brief explaining their design decisions. It is one of the highlights of the Polaris year.
